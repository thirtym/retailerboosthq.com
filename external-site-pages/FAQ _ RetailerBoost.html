Our Business Model

We make money from the margin between what we spend on ads and the commission we earn on sales we generate. It's simple: we pay Google for clicks, and when those clicks turn into sales, we earn commission from you. If the commission earned exceeds our ad spend, we're profitable on that order.
No, we operate on very thin margins by design. Our typical ROAS target is around 105-110%, meaning we might make just a few cents profit per order. We're not trying to bid cheap and charge high to maximize our take.

Our model is a volume game. We aim to get the most orders possible for our merchants. At scale, across many retailers, those small margins add up to make our business commercially viable.
We run a lean operation:
  • Small, efficient team

  • Data-driven bidding strategies optimized over millions of clicks
  • Portfolio approach across many merchants to smooth out variance
This allows us to operate profitably on thin margins without compromising campaign performance for our merchants.
Because it works at scale. Think of it like a portfolio strategy: some orders might not be profitable for us, but across thousands of orders and hundreds of merchants, the math works out. We're essentially betting on volume and consistency rather than maximizing profit on each individual sale.

Attribution means giving credit to the marketing channel that generated a sale. For example: if a customer clicks a RetailerBoost Google Shopping ad on Monday, browses but doesn't buy, then comes back on Wednesday and purchases, should that sale be credited to RetailerBoost? Yes! The RetailerBoost ad was the original paid touchpoint that brought them to your store. We attribute the sale to that ad click, and you pay commission on that order. If the customer had clicked your own Google Ads instead, that sale would be credited to your campaign, not RetailerBoost.
RetailerBoost uses a 30-day attribution window. If a customer clicks a RetailerBoost ad and purchases within 30 days, we attribute the sale to RetailerBoost - even if they interact with your other marketing in between. For example: if they click a RetailerBoost ad on January 1st and purchase on January 25th, the sale is attributed to RetailerBoost (within 30 days). If they purchase on February 5th, it's not attributed (beyond 30 days). This matches standard industry practices and Google Ads conversion windows.
Last-click attribution credits the last marketing touchpoint before a purchase. RetailerBoost uses a last non-direct click attribution window: if a customer clicked a RetailerBoost ad within 30 days and makes a purchase, we still attribute it - even if they interacted with your other marketing in between. However, if the customer clicks your own paid ads after clicking ours, your ad gets the attribution (not us). This ensures you never pay commission for sales driven by your own campaigns.
An order shows 'No Attribution' when: 1) Customer found you organically (Google search, direct visit, bookmark) with no ad click, 2) Ad click was more than 30 days ago (beyond attribution window), 3) Click was from a different campaign (not RetailerBoost), or 4) Tracking was blocked by ad blockers or privacy tools.
